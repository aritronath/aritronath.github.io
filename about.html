Education and Training

My education and training journey began in Holkar Science College in Indore, India (my hometown) where I earned a Bachelor of Science in Biotechnology in 2006. Next, I received a Master of Science degree in Biomedical Genetics from VIT University in Vellore, India.

I moved to the United States in 2008, where I obtained my PhD in Genetics from Michigan State University under the guidance of Prof. Christina Chan. My thesis research explored the roles of free-fatty acids in promoting the metastatic progression of liver cancer (hepatocellular carcinoma) cells. During my graduate research, I applied systems biology tools to characterize the cellular effects of elevated saturated free-fatty acids in liver cancer cell lines. My research revealed that high levels of saturated free-fatty acid palmitate can lead to the loss of cell adhesion proteins, a key indicator of cancer cells switching towards a more invasive and metastatic state. Subsequently, I found that CD36, a protein involved in fatty acid uptake, is elevated in metastatic liver cancer and other carcinomas. In addition to using cell and molecular biology tools (mammalian cell culture, western blots, qRT-PCR, flow cytometry, confocal microscopy), my graduate research introduced me to systems biology and bioinformatics research using microarray data and publicly available large-scale cancer genomics datasets (the Cancer Genome Atlas).

After completing my PhD, I joined Dr. R. Stephanie Huang's translational pharmacogenomics lab at the University of Chicago (the lab later relocated to the University of Minnesota). During my postdoctoral training, I focused on two main areas of research:
1. Developing computational tools for the characterization and study of non-coding RNAs
2. Discovering novel biomarkers of drug response using large-scale cancer drug screens.
Through my research, I found that expression levels of non-coding RNAs (eg. miRNA, lncRNA) are often missing in vast majority of transcriptomic datasets. However, high-quality datasets with complete data can be used accurately impute the expression levels of miRNAs and lncRNAs. Subsequently, I explored the potential of non-coding RNAs to serve as biomarkers of anticancer drug response. My work revealed that lncRNAs can be exceptional biomarkers for anticancer agents, often outperforming current established clinical biomarkers.
